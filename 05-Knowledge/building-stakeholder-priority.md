# Building Stakeholder Priority Map

Created: 2026-06-27
Project context: Fridson — office lifecycle / facility reporting and maintenance intelligence

## Purpose

This note maps the main stakeholders around a building by two dimensions:

- **Value to the building**: how much the stakeholder contributes to the building's financial, operational, safety, or experiential performance.
- **Interest / priority in the building**: how strongly the stakeholder cares about the building's condition, usability, costs, risks, or long-term performance.

## Stakeholder table

| Stakeholder | Role in the building ecosystem | Value to building | Interest / priority level | Main interest | Why they matter |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| **Building owner / landlord** | Owns the asset | Very high | Very high | Asset value, rent income, capex, compliance, long-term performance | Final decision-maker or key economic stakeholder |
| **Asset manager / investor** | Manages the building as an investment | Very high | Very high | NOI, occupancy, operating costs, risk, ESG, asset appreciation | Cares about financial performance and strategic improvements |
| **Facility manager / property manager** | Runs day-to-day operations | Very high | Very high | Maintenance, tenant satisfaction, vendor coordination, compliance | Usually the operational buyer or power user for building-management tools |
| **Tenants / occupiers** | Rent and use the space | High | Very high | Comfort, safety, service quality, rent/service charge fairness | Their satisfaction affects retention, complaints, and revenue |
| **Building users / employees / residents** | Experience the building daily | Medium-high | High | Easy issue reporting, comfort, cleanliness, safety | They generate real-time signals about problems in the building |
| **Maintenance team / technicians** | Fix building issues | High | High | Clear work orders, asset info, access, prioritization | They determine how quickly problems are resolved |
| **External vendors / service providers** | Deliver specialist services | Medium-high | Medium-high | SLAs, contracts, access, scheduling, payment | Essential for HVAC, elevators, cleaning, security, fire systems, and similar services |
| **Compliance / safety inspectors** | Enforce rules and standards | High | Medium-high | Fire safety, accessibility, legal compliance, documentation | Can create serious risk if requirements are not met |
| **Insurance providers** | Cover building risk | Medium-high | Medium | Risk reduction, incident records, maintenance proof | Their requirements can influence safety investments and documentation |
| **Energy / ESG managers** | Optimize sustainability performance | Medium-high | Medium-high | Energy use, emissions, reporting, certifications | Increasingly important for cost control, regulation, and investor appeal |
| **Security teams / emergency services** | Protect people and property | High | Medium | Access control, incidents, evacuation, emergency readiness | Critical during incidents, but less involved in daily operations |
| **Municipality / regulators** | Oversee public rules and permits | Medium-high | Medium | Zoning, building codes, environmental impact, public safety | Important for legal permission to operate or modify the building |
| **Visitors / guests** | Temporary users of the space | Low-medium | Medium | Wayfinding, safety, cleanliness, comfort | Limited influence, but they affect perception of the building |
| **Neighbors / local community** | Affected by the building externally | Low-medium | Low-medium | Noise, traffic, safety, appearance, environmental effects | Usually indirect stakeholders unless the building causes disruption |

## Priority matrix

| Priority group | Stakeholders | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1: Highest priority** | Owner / landlord, asset manager, facility / property manager, tenants | They combine high financial or operational value with high interest in the building's performance |
| **Tier 2: Operationally critical** | Building users, maintenance teams, vendors, ESG / energy managers | They influence daily performance, issue resolution, sustainability, and future optimization |
| **Tier 3: Risk and compliance stakeholders** | Inspectors, regulators, insurance, security / emergency services | They may not use the building daily, but can create major risk or cost if ignored |
| **Tier 4: Peripheral stakeholders** | Visitors, neighbors, local community | Lower direct influence, but still relevant for reputation, experience, and public impact |

## Fridson focus chain

For Fridson, the strongest initial product narrative should focus on the chain from occupant problem signal to operational resolution and owner-level insight.

| Stakeholder | Pain | Fridson value |
|---|---|---|
| **Occupant / tenant** | Reporting issues is annoying, fragmented, or unclear | Scan QR → report instantly, no login required |
| **Facility manager** | Issues arrive through too many channels and lack structure | One structured intake flow with smart routing |
| **Technician / vendor** | Work orders lack context, location, and priority | Photos, location, category, priority, and issue history |
| **Owner / asset manager** | Hard to see recurring problems, costs, and asset health trends | Maintenance analytics, documentation, and asset insights |

## Product implication

Fridson should not initially try to serve every building stakeholder equally. The sharpest wedge is:

1. **Win the occupant front door** with zero-login QR reporting.
2. **Reduce facility-manager admin load** through structured intake and routing.
3. **Improve technician/vendor execution** with context-rich work orders.
4. **Create owner-level value over time** through maintenance history, recurring issue analysis, and asset-level insights.
